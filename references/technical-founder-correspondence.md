# Technical founder correspondence

Use this reference for founder DMs, prospect chats, sales engineering replies, and other human-facing technical messages where product details or performance claims matter.

## Core rule

Separate technical truth from wording.

1. The domain owner approves the substance.
2. The writer improves clarity, rhythm, and natural voice without changing that substance.

Do not use polished prose to hide uncertainty. Public marketing copy, product pages, and repository feature lists do not prove that a private implementation detail is live, available to this prospect, or measurable as described.

## Workflow

### 1. Capture the prospect's actual questions

Preserve their order when they ask numbered questions. Record their current stack, named problem, buying context, and unknowns. Do not restate every detail in the reply unless confirmation is needed.

### 2. Build an internal claim matrix

Classify each proposed technical statement:

- **Confirmed live:** the domain owner confirms it is operating now and available in this context.
- **Measured:** evidence exists with a named method and scope.
- **Planned:** not live; describe it only as planned.
- **Unknown:** ask the domain owner or the prospect. Do not fill the gap from general knowledge.

Claims about topology, data sources, routing, preconfirmations, sender paths, performance, benchmarks, availability, and roadmap normally require domain-owner confirmation.

### 3. Get truth approval before prose optimization

If a domain owner has not reviewed the technical substance, return a claim matrix or clarification questions instead of a send-ready message.

If the domain owner corrects a draft, treat the corrected version as the factual base. Do not reintroduce removed details while making it sound better.

### 4. Write like a founder in a live chat

First identify the correspondence stage. A cold first touch and an active technical thread need different shapes.

For a cold first touch:

- Anchor the opening in one verified public artifact or concrete observed fact. A segment label, inferred pain, or generic praise is not evidence.
- Ask one relevant technical question about the prospect's current path.
- State the sender's relationship to the product plainly. Do not use a fake self-introduction or corporate third-person voice when writing from the founder's own account.
- Offer one contextual next step only when it is actually available.
- Default to no links in the body. Keep proof and commercial URLs as follow-up metadata until the prospect's reply makes one relevant.
- Do not dump pricing, plan names, feature lists, proof links, and signup links into the first message.
- Do not sign a direct message with the sender's own name unless the channel genuinely needs a signature.

For an active thread:

- Use contractions and plain terms.
- Keep some natural roughness. Do not turn a DM into a white paper.
- Match the prospect's structure when useful.
- Avoid generic praise openers, consultant transitions, and polished recap sections.
- Do not add a benchmark, endpoint, trial, metric pack, or CTA unless the technical owner confirmed it can be offered.
- Close with one natural action or question.

The goal is credible and human, not intentionally sloppy. Fix ambiguity and awkward wording, but do not sand every sentence into the same cadence.

### 4a. Check the campaign as a batch

A message can pass an anti-slop scan by itself while the campaign still reads automated. Before approving a batch:

1. Scan each message separately for local defects.
2. Concatenate only the message bodies into a temporary file and scan the batch.
3. Inspect repeated sentence openers, repeated questions, identical founder-positioning lines, identical CTAs, and dominant sentence-length rhythms.
4. Vary those components independently. Do not choose all variants from one weak modulo or character-sum selector, which can collide across similar handles. Use a stable digest or another deterministic distributor when reproducibility matters.
5. Re-scan the batch. A passing individual score does not override a failing batch score.

Variation must not weaken truth or targeting. Evidence, the technical question, and the available next step remain prospect-specific even when the phrasing changes.

### 5. Preserve communication state

Distinguish:

- drafted;
- sent;
- deleted or retracted;
- corrected replacement pending;
- replacement sent;
- prospect accepted or declined the action.

A deleted message stays in history but its claims and CTA are inactive. Do not report a benchmark, trial, or offer as awaiting the prospect when the message proposing it was deleted.

## Common failure modes

- Inferring private technical capabilities from public product copy.
- Producing a confident point-by-point answer before the technical owner responds.
- Adding precise benchmark metrics because they sound credible, without confirming the test can be run.
- Optimizing an inaccurate draft instead of correcting its factual base.
- Treating a deleted message as a completed prospect touch.
- Rewriting a domain owner's correction so heavily that the meaning changes.

## Final check

Before delivery, verify:

- every technical claim is confirmed live, measured, clearly planned, or removed;
- superiority claims have evidence and scope;
- the message sounds like a direct conversation, not generated sales copy;
- wording changes did not alter the approved facts;
- the CTA is technically available and currently active;
- CRM or relationship state matches what was actually sent.
